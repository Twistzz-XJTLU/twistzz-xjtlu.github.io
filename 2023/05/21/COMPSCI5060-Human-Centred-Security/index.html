What is Human-Centered Security?

Aim of Human-Centered Security: To make privacy and security an integrated, natural, unburdened part of Human-Computer interaction

Key challenges:

  • Security concepts are complicated for the average user
  • Security is a secondary task
  • Human capabilities are limited
  • Misaligned priorities
  • Habituation
  • New threats everyday

Introduction to Security

What is Computer Security?

Protecting systems against misuse and interference in order to preserve confidentiality, integrity, and availability of system resources.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality: covers two concepts

  • Data confidentiality: private or confidential information is not made available or disclosed to unauthorized individuals.
    • E.g., Universities keep grades confidential.
  • Privacy: an individual’s data is used/disclosed according to a set of rules.
    • E.g., Mobile app can access user’s location but not camera feed.
  • Other related concepts:
    • Secrecy: keep data hidden.
      • E.g., lawyer keeps communications with his client secret.
    • Anonymity: keep identity hidden.
      • E.g., person uses Mr_Smurf as a nickname and refrains from uploading a personal photo on an online forum.

Integrity

  • Data integrity: information and programs can only be changed via an authorized operation.
    • E.g., no one can change your Facebook profile picture except you.
  • System integrity: systems consistently perform their meant function, without intended or unintended unauthorized manipulation.
    • E.g., deleting a tweet does not result in deleting another tweet

Availability

Availability: systems and data are promptly usable to authorized users.

  • E.g., the system shouldn’t prevent a lecturer from entering student grades

What do attackers do?

  • They exploit bugs to:
    • disclose data to breach confidentiality
    • alter data to breach integrity
    • deny services to breach availability
  • How do they exploit bugs?
    • Software and Hardware bugs (e.g., backdoor, network vulnerability)
    • Humans (e.g., social engineering)
    • Side channels (e.g., observations, heat traces, smudges)

Threat Modelling

Perfect security is not possible

Attempting to protect against everything is too expensive, too inconvenient, and sometimes not worth the effort → prioritize!

Identify most likely ways system will be attacked

  • Identify likely attackers and their resources, e.g., lone wolf, organized hackers, nation-sponsored?
  • Identify consequences of the attack, e.g., embarrassment, bankruptcy, fatal?
  • Attackers will exploit the weakest link in the system

Cryptography can help achieve many security properties

Cryptography basics

Why use ciphers?

  • Protect confidentiality
    • Encrypt files (e.g., photos, documents).
    • Encrypt emails, personal messages, and chat logs.
    • Encrypt all data that is sent and received when browsing the internet.
  • Verify integrity
    • Verify that the message was not tampered.
  • Authenticity
    • Verify source of information (e.g., email sender, website owner).
  • Ensure secrecy
    • Only the receiver can decrypt the message.

Humans

Why do Humans use computers?

  • Interfaces should stay as invisible as possible so users can focus on the task rather on the interface.
  • How?
    • User’s side: understand the abilities and limitations of users, and then build technologies that leverage their capabilities, and also avoid things they are incapable of doing.
    • Technology side: design with what we’ve learned about the users’ in mind, and then evaluate how well this technology works.

Bad design costs time, money and lives!

Human-Computer Interaction

Human-computer interaction is a discipline concerned with the design, evaluation, and implementation of interactive computing systems for human use and with the study of major phenomena surrounding them

There are many methodologies and approaches for designing interaction:

  • User-centered Design
  • Particpatory Design
  • Value Sensitive Design
  • Activity-centered Design
  • Process-centered Design

User-centered Design

An iterative design process that puts the user at its center.

  • Understand the Context of use
  • Understand the Requirements
  • Design Solutions
  • Evaluate the Solutions

Prototyping

  • Prototyping refers to rapidly creating an approximation of a design.
  • Prototypes embody design hypotheses and enable designers to test their hypotheses.
  • It structures innovation, collaboration and creativity in design.
  • The goal in prototyping is not the artefact – it’s feedback and iteration

Why prototype

  • Gain insights into user behavior.
  • Communicate ideas to other teammates and stakeholders
  • Collect initial data to support design choice.
  • Reflect on the product

Lessons from those prototypes

  • Prototypes are almost never complete
  • Goal is to simulate specific aspects of the design in order to improve our understanding of those aspects

Prototypes help you find:

  • Known unknowns: aspects that you know you don’t understand and want to learn about.
    • E.g., is this warning message intuitive?
  • Unknown unknowns: aspects that you don’t know are open issues.
    • E.g., why aren’t older adults using password managers?

Prototyping allows collecting feedback from..

  • Colleagues
  • Clients (Company)
  • Yourself
  • Users

How to prototype?

  • Determine the fidelity of the prototype
  • The fidelity of a prototype refers to how it conveys the look-and-feel of the final product (basically, its level of detail and realism).
  • Fidelity can vary in the areas of:
    • Visual design
    • Content
    • Interactivity
  • Prototypes can range between two extremes:
    • Low-Fidelity
    • High-Fidelity

Low-fidelity prototypes

  • Paper-based or using wireframe software (e.g., moqups.com).

  • Can also be made of cardboard if form factor is important.

  • A human simulates interactivity (we’ll discuss the Wizard of Oz approach in a bit).

  • Faster to build

    • Sketching is faster than programming

  • Easier to Change

    • Easy to make changes between tests (sometimes during tests)
    • No code investments

  • Focus attention on big picture

    • Designer doesn’t waste time on detail
    • Customer can make some creative suggestions

+ Inexpensive

+ Fast

+ Allows changes even during experiments

- Uncertainty about technical feasibility

- Limited and unnatural interactivity

High-fidelity prototypes

  • Involve programming
  • Gives the user the impression that it is a real system
  • Many hardcoded details
  • Interactive

+ Meaningful feedback for usability evaluation

+ Allows testing UI elements and/or interactions

+ More convincing to clients

- Higher cost

- Relatively slower to build

- Iterations can be costly

Medium-fidelity prototypes

Somewhere in between

Prototyping guidelines

  • Prototypes should be disposable
  • They are not required to be complete
  • They do not need to be updated
  • Should be easy to change
  • During prototyping, “possibility to change” narrow as fidelity increases
  • Build LOTS of prototypes

How do we evaluate the prototypes?

Evaluating prototypes

  1. Define tasks for participants
  2. Plan ahead the procedure of the study
  3. Plan ahead which information will be gathered (more about this in the next lecture)

Roles of team members:

  • A member will be a Facilitator: this member will introduce the system and introduce the tasks to the participant.
  • In case of paper prototypes: Another member will be a Computer, their job will be to simulate how the system would react to the user’s actions. Fun fact: this type of experiments is called Wizard of Oz experiment

Guidelines:

  • Give clear concise tasks
  • Don’t tell the participant how to do the task, focus on what they need to do.
  • Don’t give any hints, object, or criticize the participant.
  • Don’t prime participants (e.g., oh I worked so hard on this prototype, now tell me what you think  this is wrong).
  • Observe the participant, take notes, even record a video and take pictures if the participant consents.
  • Encourage think aloud to know why the participant is doing what they’re doing, and ask questions to better understand.

Usability

What makes something usable?

  • Effectiveness: how completely and accurately the task is completed.
  • Error tolerance: how well the system prevents errors and can help users recover from them.
  • Transparency: how prominent the system is. Ideally, an interface to a task should be as invisible as possible.
  • Efficiency (speed): how quickly can the user complete the task.
  • Learnability: how easy/fast can users learn how to use the system. Ideally systems are intuitive and do not require learning.
  • Memorability: this extends learnability – users should be able to remember how to use the system without high demand.
  • User preference: how much users like the system – do they enjoy using it?

Often times you cannot achieve all of that at the same time. You can have good usability on some factors but not others. Sometimes there is even a trade off.

Make the system among us as many of these factors as possible

Usability of Android Patterns

  • Error tolerance: how well the system prevents errors and can help users recover from them. very low

  • Learnability: how easy/fast can users learn how to use the system. Ideally systems are intuitive and do not require learning. very high
  • Memorability: this extends learnability – users should be able to remember how to use the system without high demand. very high
  • User preference: how much users like the system – do they enjoy using it? high

The 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design by Jakob Nielsen

  1. Visibility of system status
  2. Match between system and the real world
  3. User control and freedom
  4. Consistency and standards
  5. Error prevention
  6. Recognition rather than recall
  7. Flexibility and efficiency of use
  8. Aesthetic and minimalist design
  9. Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors
  10. Help and documentation

How to use all of that

  • The heuristics can be used for evaluating existing systems
  • They can also be used when designing a new system, to check the compliance of the designs

Why is usable security challenging?

  • It is very common that usability improvements result in less security.
  • It is very common that security improvements result in less usability.
  • This led to a long discussion among researchers and practitioners on the trade-off between Usability and Security.
  • IMPORTANT: While the trade-off is sometimes unavoidable, we should not take it for granted that security will reduce usability.
  • Remember last lecture: if a security system is not usable, people will not use it, ultimately leading to less security.
  • Both usability and security should be integrated from the beginning of the design process.

Usability of Security Systems

Whitten and Tygar argue that security software is usable if the people who are expected to use it:

  • are reliably made aware of the security tasks they need to perform
  • are able to figure out how to successfully perform those tasks;
  • don’t make dangerous errors; and
  • are sufficiently comfortable with the interface to continue using it.

Behavioral Research

  • Early research in Human-Centered Security and Human-Computer Interaction borrowed concepts from behavioral sciences (e.g., psychology, cognitive science, etc.)
  • Types of behavioral research
    • Descriptive research
    • Relational research
    • Experimental research

Descriptive behavioral research

  • Aim: describe a situation or set of events
  • Claim: X is happening
  • Example: In a group of 10 users, 8 of which play games on their mobile phones and authenticate by PIN, and 2 of which do not play games and authenticate by lock patterns
  • Interesting observation, but does not establish a relationship between playing games and authentication
  • Does not explain why this happens
  • Output is often qualitative

Methods:

  • Interviews
  • Focus groups
  • Observations
  • Field studies

Relational behavioral research

  • Aim: Identify relations between multiple variables
  • Methods: observations, field studies, surveys
  • Claim: X is related to Y
  • Example: a relationship exists between number of hours spent playing an online game, and typing speed (confirmed with a statistical test such as Pearson correlation)
  • What can we learn from that?
    • It is possible that playing this online game increases typing speed
    • It is possible that users who type quickly tend to enjoy the online game
    • It is possible that there’s a different third factor, that causes users to both: type quickly and play online games
  • Relational research does not explain causality (why this happens)
  • Output can be qualitative or quantitative

Methods

Interviews, focus groups, field studies, observations, and also questionnaires:

Experimental research

  • Aim: Identify causes of a situation or event
  • Methods: controlled experiments
  • Claim: X is responsible for Y
  • Example: a group of users enters 20 PINs, another group of users enters 20 Patterns. The mean authenticate time is shorter for the group that used Patterns (confirmed by statistical tests).
  • What can we learn from that?
    • Authentication using Patterns is faster than when using PINs
  • Output can be qualitative or quantitative

How to design and run a user study?

  1. Define the goal of the study
  2. Define a threat model
  3. Identify the measures (aka dependent variables)
  4. Identify the independent variables
  5. Identify the design structure: between-, within-, or mixed-subjects
  6. Define the procedure and user tasks
  7. Plan ahead for the analysis
  8. Conduct the [pilot-]study
  9. Analyze the results

Defining the Goal of the Experiment

  • Define a clear goal for the study

    • Example: evaluate the usability of an authentication scheme
    • Example: evaluate the security of an authentication scheme

  • Define a hypothesis

    • Example: PINs are more secure against shoulder surfing compared to Patterns

Defining a Threat Model

  • A threat model is a sample scenario for an attack

  • Assume best case scenario for the attacker, and worst case scenario for the user

  • Be as detailed as possible

  • Why do we define a threat model?

    • Set the scene
    • Set the focus of the evaluation (sometimes it is impossible to consider all possible threats)
    • Inform the design decisions of the security evaluation

  • Thermal attacks

  • Smudge attacks

  • Shoulder surfing

Sample threat model:

A smartphone user enters her PIN in a public setting. An observer (attacker) standing in the user’s vicinity has perfect sight on the display (no reflections, no occlusions) […] After observing the PIN-entry, the attacker gets in possession of the device and tries to replicate the observed input. The observation attack takes place without additional equipment (e.g. camcorder).

Insider Threat Model

  • Insiders are on the inside, with access privileges.
  • They are trusted.
  • Aware of certain details (e.g., policies, procedures & technology).
  • Know where the valuable data is and how to access it.
Three categories
  • Malicious insider: someone taking advantage of their access to inflict harm, examples:
    • Family member spies on another family member’s phone.
    • Employee in an organization exploits their access rights to do something malicious.
  • Negligent insiders: someone inadvertently makes errors and/or disregards policies, which place others at risk, examples:
    • Family member lets a burglar into their house.
    • Employee does not follow security policies, resulting in a security breach.
  • Infiltrators: external attacker that obtains legitimate access credentials without authorization, examples:
    • A visitor to an organization retrieve an employee’s credentials and uses them to access the organization’s data.
    • A former employee leaves malicious code in the network before leaving a company to allow him to infiltrate the company’s network after he leaves.

Experiment Design Structure

There are different ways to design experiments:

  • Between-subject design
  • Within-subject design
  • Mixed-subject design (combination of the above two)

Between subject design

Advantages

  • Users do not learn from different conditions
  • The experiment requires less time per participant
  • Issues such as fatigue and frustration are minimal

Disadvantages

  • Susceptible to individual differences (can introduce noise to the collected data)
  • It requires a larger number of participants in order to eliminate the noise (e.g., 20 participants per condition)

Within subject design

Advantages

  • Requires a smaller sample size (e.g., 20 participants in total)
  • Easier to conduct
  • More economical

Disadvantages

  • The results might be biased due to learning effects
  • The results might be biased due to fatigue

Counterbalancing conditions

  • If all participants go through the conditions in the same order, there might be biases
  • Solution: counter balance the conditions; i.e. change the order of conditions for each participant

How to counter balance?

  • Randomize the order of conditions (not always a good idea)
  • Use an NxN Latin Square (where N is the number of conditions)

Defining a procedure

  • A study procedure is a walkthrough the study
  • It is always better to write the procedure before doing the study
    • This allows you to spot any problems in your procedure or design
    • It acts like a todo list for what you need to do during the study

Example

Planning ahead for the analysis

  • Make sure you log all the data needed for the analysis
  • It is always safer to:
    • Store fine-grained data (e.g., instead of calculating the entry time in real time and logging it, log the start time, and the end time, then calculate the entry time post hoc)
    • Store the data in the lowest magnitude possible (e.g., entry time in milliseconds rather than in seconds)
  • Do a pilot test, and analyze the data.

User study Dos and Don’ts

  • Get the participant’s consent
  • Do not expose them to any risks greater than those encountered in their normal working life
  • Allow them to withdraw at any time
  • Allow them to ask questions
  • Do a pilot study first
  • Do not influence them
  • Store data securely and anonymously

Conduct the [pilot-]study

Analyzing the results

  • Descriptive analysis
    • Start by calculating the means, and the standard deviation
    • When dealing with ordinal data, it is better to use medians
    • However comparing means does not tell us much
      • Example: if the mean running speed of 3 Italians is higher than the mean running speed of 3 Austrians → this does not mean that Italians are always faster
  • Statistical analysis
    • Statistical tests tell us the probability that the difference between the means occurs by chance
    • If the probability is less than 5% (p < 0.05) then we can assume that the difference between the two means is statistically significant. And only then we can generalize our claims.

History of Passwords

Passwords are staying for a while

Benefits

Service providers

  • Low costs
  • Easy to implement
  • Replaceable when compromised
  • Revocable by administrator
  • Enforceable policies

User

  • Fast entry on desktops
  • Most users already familiarized
  • Easy to learn
  • Shareable with others
  • High degree of control/freedom

Others

  • Independent of identification

Drawbacks

Service providers

  • Large number of attack vectors
  • Anomaly detection is costly
  • Attacks are simple to carry out
  • Attack automation is simple
  • Attacks can have severe consequences

User

  • Memory overload from too many passwords
  • Suboptimal coping strategies
  • Stronger passwords difficult to memorize
  • Entry on mobile devices is difficult
  • Disliked by users / perceived as a burden

Others

  • Weak passwords are a risk for users and service providers

Why do we use passwords?

  • Security: to protect access to a system
  • User: to access a system

The users’ preferences are

  • Pick passwords that are easy to remember
  • Force them to come up with something hard to remember, and they’ll forget them, reset them too often, or write them down.

List of most common passwords

How are passwords typically attacked?

  • Offline attacks
  • Online attacks
  • Side channel attacks
  • Social engineering
  • Malwares and eavesdropping

Offline attacks

  • Usually large scale attacks.
  • Attackers retrieve a database of passwords (e.g., by exploiting backdoors, network vulnerabilities, insider attacks, or malware), and then they analyse it offline.

How are passwords stored?

Hashing

  • Takes an input (e.g., password) and turns it into a string of text that always has the same length.

  • There are many hashing functions e.g., MD5, SHA1, SHA2, SHA3, etc..

  • You never need the passwords in plain text.

  • Once the user provides their password, hash it using the same function used to hash the password on creation.

  • Then compare the input hash with the stored hash.

  • You can further improve hashing by using salts!

How attackers break Hashes/password dumps?

  • Brute force guesses → try all combinations
  • Rainbow tables → tables that contain previously hashed texts
  • Common word attacks
  • Dictionary word attacks

How can we get passwords for analysis?

  • Real leaked databases (yes many of them are online, and no, not only on the dark web).
  • User studies (very tricky due to ethical concerns and responsibility to protect passwords).

Online attacks

  • Usually limited because the attacker is blocked after many failed attempts.
  • Exploiting fall back authentication (e.g., secret questions).

Side channel attacks

  • Shoulder surfing
  • Thermal attacks
  • Smudge attacks

Social engineering

  • Asking people for their passwords
  • Phishing attacks

Malwares and eavesdropping

  • Install a keylogger
  • Sniff packets exchanged on a network

How can we make passwords stronger?

  • Password policies
  • Password meters
  • Passphrases
  • DO NOT REUSE PASSWORDS!
  • Make your passwords long and unpredictable.
  • Use password managers.
  • Two-factor authentication
  • Multi-factor authentication

What is user authentication and why do we need it?

  • User Authentication is the process of proving a Human User’s identity to a computer system.
  • Is there anything better than passwords for authentication?

Authentication Factors

  • Knowledge factor
    • Authenticate using something you know
    • Examples: PINs, Text passwords, Android lock patterns
  • Possession factor
    • Authenticate using something you have
    • Examples: Keys, Smart IDs, ATM cards
  • Inherence factor (i.e., biometric)
    • Authenticate using something you are
    • Examples: Fingerprint, facial recognition, iris scaning

Knowledge-based Authentication

  • Alphanumeric passwords
  • Graphical passwords

Graphical passwords can be classified to

  • Recall: Drawing a picture, tracing a pattern, tapping specific points
  • Cued-recall: Drawing or taping on top of an image cue
  • Recognition: Recognizing images.

Graphical passwords

  • Advantages
    • Images and visual patterns may be easier to remember than characters
    • Pointing/clicking/tapping/drawing can sometimes be easier/faster than typing
    • Sometimes more appealing/fun than text passwords
    • May be harder to store or share password
    • Less susceptible to phishing attacks
  • Disadvantages
    • Doesn’t work for vision impaired
    • Requires a screen (sometimes of high resolution and color)
    • Some types particularly vulnerable to shoulder surfing
    • May have very small password space
    • User chosen systems vulnerable to predictable user behavior

Knowledge-based Authentication

  • Requires memorizing/remembering something
  • Might separate identification and verification
  • What are its advantages?
    • Humans are capable of remembering things, but not complicated things.
    • Usually inexpensive (e.g. does not require special sensors).
    • Changeable.
    • Usually easy to learn.
    • Nothing to carry.
    • Familiar.
    • Relatively easy to deploy.
  • What are its disadvantages?
    • If the user can remember it, so can the adversary.
    • Demand a high level of memorability.
    • Potentially guessable without computers.
  • Usually these authentication schemes need to be sufficiently random to resist guesses, but at the same time not too difficult for users to generate and remember

Possession-based Authentication

  • Requires carrying something
  • Requires a mechanism to verify the token
  • What are its advantages?
    • Easily explainable to users.
    • Sometimes can be remotely deactivated.
  • What are its disadvantages?
    • Might be expensive to replace.
    • Can be lost.
    • Might take time to realize it was lost.
    • It can be stolen.

Biometric Authentication

  • Relies on something in the user’s biology
  • Further classification
    • Physiological
    • Behavioral

Physiological Biometric Authentication

  • Relies on passive features in the human.
  • A bodily measurement that is taken from users, which does not necessarily require an action by the user.
Fingerprint
  • Advantages
    • Reasonably unique.
    • Very fast and easy to use/learn.
    • No need to memorize or carry anything.
  • Disadvantages
    • Cannot be changed.
    • Doesn’t work when your finger is wet or dirty.
    • Users can authenticate without their consent.’
  • Readers can be optical
  • or capacitive

Face recognition

  • Same advantages as fingerprint.
  • Similar disadvantages, but might not work well with certain clothing, accessories and make up.
  • Can be detected using RGB (i.e., video) cameras
    • Example: Face unlock on older Android phones
    • Relies on face identifiers (e.g. distance between the eyes or between your nose and upper lips).
    • Can be fooled with pictures!
    • Doesn’t work when face is hidden or in dark areas.
  • Can also be detected using Depth cameras
    • Example: FaceID on iPhone X.
    • Projects infrared patterns on your face.
    • Works in the dark and through eye wear.
Voice recognition
  • Doesn’t work well when it is noisy.
  • Doesn’t work well if you sound different.

Behavioral Biometric Authentication

  • Relies on active continuous features in the human’s behaviour
  • The user has to be actively doing something.

Behavioral biometric authentication relies on distinguishing the user’s unique behaviour.

  • Touch behavior
  • Gaze behaviour
  • Typing behavior
  • Ways people perform gestures
  • Lip movement behaviour
  • Gait
  • Handwriting
  • Heart rhythm
  • High level behaviour (e.g., how hard you press the car’s pedals, which apps do you launch first, etc.)
Advantages of behavioural biometrics
  • Continuous, seamless.
  • Very effective against insider and shoulder surfing attacks
Disadvantages
  • Doesn’t work that well yet e.g., might lock you out

Biometric Authentication

General advantages of biometric authentication:

  • Fast.
  • Easy to use.
  • Easy to learn.
  • Reasonably unique.
  • No need to memorize or carry anything.
  • People like it and perceive it to be secure.
  • [Cannot be written down or shared].

General disadvantages of biometric authentication:

  • It is challenging to balance usability and security (false positives vs false negatives).
  • May be difficult to change.
  • Often results in trusting third party companies with biometric data.
  • High accuracy requires expensive and large special equipment (today).
  • Some biometrics are difficult to capture under some conditions (low light, dry skin, injury, etc.).
  • Some biometrics change over time.
  • May increase value of a person’s body parts to an attacker.
  • May be difficult cancel (no explicit consent from the user).
  • May leak personal information.
  • Can be leaked from companies (see Zhang et al. Fingerprints On Mobile Devices: Abusing and leaking)
  • They cannot be invalidated.

Multi-factor Authentication

  • Adds a second layer of protection e.g., you must have the ATM card, and you must know the PIN.
  • Usually does not rely on human memory
  • Examples
  • Disadvantages
    • There is some evidence that if u give users a second means of authentication beside passwords, they’ll choose weaker passwords.
    • Slows down login process – some are slower than others.
    • Hardware tokens cost money, and are inconvenient to carry
    • Prone to some attacks like social engineering, man-in-the-middle attacks, and phone hijacking.

Fall back authentication

  • A mechanism to avoid locking you out in case you forget your password.
  • Sometimes it is needed if you use a system infrequently, or if you recently changed your password.
  • Needed in case admin password resets are essential.
  • Examples
    • Reset link sent via email or SMS
    • IT Help desk
    • Secret question
      • Very bad idea!
      • Has low entropy (e.g., what’s your favorite color?).
      • Can be leaked through social media (e.g., where is your grandmother’s birthplace?).
      • Acquaintances can often guess answers to secret questions.
  • Lock out users who make incorrect but popular guesses
  • Remove most easily guessed questions
  • Disallow popular answers
  • Occasionally ask secret questions after user has logged in successfully
  • NIST recommends not to use secret questions!
  • Other ways
    • Requiring PIN when fingerprint is not working.
    • Reduces security to PINs (i.e., observable, guessable, etc.)

Context-based authentication

  • The ubiquity of sensors allows us to track many things about the user
  • Authentication can be customized based on the context.

That’s quite a bunch authentication methods.. Now which should we use?

It depends on what you’re optimizing against!

Examples

  • If you’re worried that these types of attacks could occur, then do not use knowledge-based authentication.
  • If you’re worried if someone could forcibly authenticate you against your will, then do not use biometric authentication.
  • If encouraging usage is more important than security, then focus on usability (e.g., contactless payments offered by credit card companies).
  • If security is prioritized over costs, then focus on usable security (e.g., Software Engineers in Fortune 500 companies use 2-factor authentication).

Week6 Guidelines for Usable Security

Content

  • What happens after authentication?
  • Access control
  • Usable and secure authorization
  • Usable and secure communication
  • Usable and secure interface design
  • How to design a usable and secure system

What happens after authentication?

  • Authentication aims to keep unauthorized people out.
  • But even after a user has authenticated, the system still needs to maintain security
  • A secure system should:
    • Allow users to do what they need to accomplish their tasks.
    • Do so while protecting the security of the user’s computer and the larger system they are interacting with.
  • After a user is properly authenticated, there are still many security issues that could come up: e.g., phishing, viruses and spyware, etc. all of these can compromise the system by bypassing authentication.
  • A core contributor to these problems is when computers fail to behave in a way that the user expects or understands.
  • Designing secure systems that are usable can prevent this from happening

Answer

  • Authorization, permissions and access control

  • Maintaining usability and security

  • Identification

    • Claim identity, e.g., ask for username

  • Authentication

    • Verify identity, e.g., ask for password

  • Authorization

    • Give access to resource, e.g., read/delete own messages, but not messages of other users

What is authorization?

  • Authorization is the process of establishing if an entity (e.g., a user that has already authenticated, or a system), is permitted to have access to a resource.
  • Authorization determines what a subject is and is not allowed to do.
  • Access control: the process of enforcing the required security for a particular resource.

Access Control

  • Selectively allowing and restricting subjects to access objects
    • Subject: the entity requesting access to a resource (e.g., user, program, system)
    • Object: the resource that the subject attempts to access (e.g., access to a data, permission to modify a file, etc.)
  • Aims to protect Confidentiality and Integrity
  • Examples of access control in the real world?
    • File permissions: the right to create, read, edit or delete a file.
    • Program permissions: the right to execute a program.
    • Social networks: who sees what.
    • Smartphone app permissions: which sensor data can this app access.
  • Least privilege philosophy: a subject is granted permissions that are needed to accomplish required tasks and nothing more.
  • Information leak: Lack of controls results in giving access to people that do not need access.
    • Example: a nurse needs data about a patient’s health, but not about the insurance.
    • Example: a file can only be viewed, but not copied.

Access Control Patterns

  • Mandatory access control
    • Resources are assigned labels. Labels of subjects are matched with labels of objects to determine access. Example: Military information.
  • Discretionary access control
    • Resource owners assign access permissions to individuals and groups. Example: file systems in Windows and MacOS.
  • Role-based access control
    • Individuals assigned roles, and permissions assigned to roles. Example: employees of a company have privileges according to their job responsibilities
  • Rule-based access control
    • Properties of the access request are checked against rules. Example: block specific IP addresses from accessing a port.
  • Distributed access control
    • Certificates used to grant access and delegate the ability to grant access, certificates must be presented to construct proof that access is permitted

A-priori policy not good enough

  • People don’t feel as much in control when they set policy up front
  • People like to be asked permission
    • “I’m very willing to be open with people, I think I’d just like the courtesy of someone asking me.”
  • People want to know both who is accessing files and why
  • People want to review accesses, revise policy

Guidelines for Usable and Secure Authorization by Ka-Ping Yee

  • Match the most comfortable way to do tasks with the least granting of authority

  • Grant authority to others in accordance with user actions indicating consent.

  • Offer the user ways to reduce others’ authority to access the user’s resources.

  • Maintain accurate awareness of others’ authority as relevant to user decisions.

  • Maintain accurate awareness of the user’s own authority to access resources.

Guidelines for Usable and Secure Communication by Ka-Ping Yee

  • Protect the user’s channels to agents that manipulate authority on the user’s behalf.

Guidelines for Usable and Secure Interfaces by Ka-Ping Yee

  • Enable the user to express safe security policies in terms that fit the user’s task.

  • Draw distinctions among objects and actions along boundaries relevant to the task.

  • Present objects and actions using distinguishable, truthful appearances.

  • Indicate clearly the consequences of decisions that the user is expected to make.

Guideline 1. Match the most comfortable way to do tasks with the least granting of authority.

Goal: create a system where the easiest, most natural, and most comfortable way the user would do a task, is also the way that is most secure.

How to Design a Usable and Secure System?

  • Security and Usability diverge when security is presented as a secondary task to the user.
    • Example: No one ever gets a computer with the idea of bringing it home, sitting on the couch and installing a patch
  • Security should be integrated into the normal workflow of the user to make the system more usable.
  • Usability and Security should both be included in software from the very beginning of the design process.

Other guidelines to keep in mind

According to Whitten and Tygar, security software is usable if the people using it..

  • are reliably made aware of the security tasks they need to perform
  • are able to figure out how to successfully perform those tasks
  • don’t make dangerous errors
  • are sufficiently comfortable with the interface to continue using it

What is Social Engineering?

Social Engineers focus on

  • Goal: what do they want to achieve?
  • Receiver: Who are they going to targ
  • Message: How will they convince the person to do what they want?
  • Channel: What channel will they use?

What Vulnerabilities that Social Engineers Exploit?

  • Unclear responsibility of individuals in an organization
  • Imagined benefit or greed
  • Trust
  • Instilling haste
  • Moral responsibility
  • Empathy
  • Guilt
  • Flattery
  • Intimidation

Social Engineering Steps

  • Information Gathering
  • Pretexting
  • Elicitation
  • Manipulation

Step 1: Information Gathering

  • Social engineering needs a lot of preparation and a lot of information
  • Various ways of doing this:
    • Dumpster diving
    • Open source intelligence: using public facing sites to collect info (e.g., social networks, Linkedin, etc.
    • Malware (e.g. keylogger, screenshots of the system)
    • Shoulder surfing
    • Whois

Step 2: Pretexting

  • Practice of presenting oneself as someone else in order to obtain private information
  • Planning and research leads to success
  • Good acting
  • Build up a false sense of trust and confidence
  • Keeping it simple → more complicated makes it easy to screw up
  • Use information that needs no verification • Define the aim
  • Plan for different reactions

Step 3: Elicitation

  • The act of getting information without directly asking for it. •
  • Social engineers do this by •
    • Exploiting human nature:
    • People want to be polite
    • People want to appear well-informed
    • People want to be appreciated
    • Honest people don’t like to withhold information or lie
  • Understanding how to communicate with people
  • Adapting communication to fit situation
  • Bonding with the target
  • Staying within the character of your pretext
  • Using effective questioning techniques

Questioning techniques

  • Neutral
    • Doesn’t target the person, no leading or directing (e.g., how do you like the weather today?)
  • Open
    • Encourages a full meaningful answer using the subject’s knowledge
    • E.g., how do you feel about the election candidates?
  • Closed
    • Gives control of the conversation and allows us to lead where it may or not go
    • E.g. who are you going to vote for?
  • Leading
    • Subtly prompts the respondent to answer in a particular way
    • Effective manipulation technique
    • Do you have any problems with your boss?
    • How fast was the red the car going when it smashed into the blue car?

Pretexting vs Elicitation

  • Pretexting: taking on a persona
  • Elicitation: using this persona to extract information

Step 4: Manipulation techniques

  • Fear then instant relief technique

    • Hi this is technical support, we detected a virus that could lock all your files.

    • We need to install an anti-virus. Can you tell me your password so we can remove the virus?

  • Guilt

    • Do someone a favor and then use it against them later
    • Using a secret as blackmail or extortion

  • Foot in the Door technique

  • Ask victim to do a very small request first

  • Once they comply, follow with the real larger request.

  • Examples

    • Salesperson asks you for the weather. This makes it easier to start a conversation, and makes it more awkward to close the conversation.
    • Can you tell me the time? Can you spare some change?

The Future of Social Engineering by Sharon Conheady

  • Same tricks, new technology
  • More sophisticated and targeted attacks
  • Social networking is an enabler for social engineers
  • Technologies can improve/automate social engineering attacks
  • Social engineering as a service

Same tricks, new technology

  • 20 years ago they were using emails
  • Next they were using SMS
  • Now social networks
  • What’s next?
  • Example: Advance Fee Fraud

More sophisticated and targeted attacks

  • Example targeted phishing attacks aka spear phishing
    • Phishing attack aimed at a particular person.
    • Contains contextual content instead of random messages
    • These attacks look more genuine
    • Victims are asked to
      • Download malicious attachments
      • Reply with sensitive information
      • Click on URLs
      • Etc.

Social networking is an enabler for social engineers

  • A huge attack surface.
  • Does not only put individuals at risk, but also their affiliations and employers.
  • Quick and easy, and can even be automated.
  • Low barrier entry point (you don’t have to be tech savvy).
  • It is a safer source for the attacker: they would be relying on public available information – nothing wrong in doing that.
  • People share information about themselves and even about others on social networks.
  • Very easy to impersonate someone or set up a fake profile.
  • Example: LinkedIn

Technologies can improve/automate social engineering attacks

  • Photo editing software
    • Can be used to forge documents, IDs or business cards.
    • There are websites that do that for you!
  • Caller ID spoofing
  • Email spoofing
  • Small/hidden cameras
  • Thermal cameras

Social Engineering as a Service

  • Outsourcing social engineering through call centers.
  • There are telephone services for
    • Professional callers
    • Caller ID spoofing
    • Available to make calls in US and EU working hours
  • 7$ to 15$
  • Most of the ones you will find online are companies doing this service as a form of security consultancy.
  • But there are companies that do it for malicious intents.

Advanced Persistent Attacks

  • A stealthy computer network attack in which a person or group gains unauthorized access to a network and remains undetected for an extended period.
  • A malware can be implanted using a USB stick.

Scam baiting

  • www.419eater.com e.g.
  • Lengthy dialogues to waste scammer’s time that they then wouldn’t use on real victims.
  • A guy sent a fake passport with a photo of Star Trek’s Captain Kirk, hoping he could get arrested.

Mitigations techniques

  • Understand who are the common targets
    • Junior staff
    • Contractors
    • Admin staff
    • Support staff
  • Identify valuable assets
  • Train employees and to create awareness of potential targets of social engineers.
  • Develop scripts and policies, and stick to them no matter what.
    • Check credentials, take your time checking it.
    • Don’t let guests roam free in the building.
    • Don’t plug in USB thumb drives you find lying around.
    • Shred documents rather than just throwing them (you can get very cheap shredders).
  • Watch for out of character questions (e.g., a delivery man asks for the server room).
  • Be aware of what you’re saying (e.g., venting about a boss in a bar) → you’re giving information that helps them pretext.
  • Be aware of shoulder surfers when dealing with sensitive data.
  • Make audits and learn from them.

Audits

A security audit checks vulnerabilities against a checklist to ensure a company complies with its security policy.

An audit answers “is this company carrying out proper security practices?”

Penetration Tests

  • Identifying vulnerabilities in a particular network and attempting to exploit them to penetrate into the system.
  • Aim: to determine whether a detected vulnerability is genuine
  • A penetration tester is a person who tests for vulnerabilities or unauthorized access to systems.
  • Systems range from computer networks to physical access to locations.
  • They can utilize phishing, or other techniques such as elicitation, to gain information from unsuspecting employees to get passwords, entry into buildings, or other access into systems.
  • Answers: “where are all of the vulnerabilities and can any of these holes be maliciously exploited?”

What is Privacy?

Privacy is related to security, but they are not the same

  • Users want to protect their information.
  • Should have the right to understand what happens with their data.
  • Should have as much control as possible over how it is used.

How privacy is protected

  • Laws
  • Self regulation
  • Technology

Privacy Policies

  • Should tell users everything they need to know about how their data is collected, used and shared.
  • They should be clear to users and explain all necessary details without overwhelming them.
  • Problems:
    • We know that most people do not read them •
    • Those who read them, often do not understand them •
    • Some mobile apps have privacy policies that can be printed on >100 A4 pages!
    • Slow and hard to read → poor usability!

Informed Consent by Design

  • Users should understand what data is being collected and shared, and consent to how it’s used
  • Six components that are required for there to be informed consent in a privacy system
    • Disclosure
    • Comprehension
    • Voluntariness
    • Competence
    • Agreement
    • Minimal distraction

Five Pitfalls in Designing for Privacy

  • Concerning understanding
    • Pitfall 1: Obscuring Potential Information Flow
      • Avoidance: clear concise statements about what is being done with the data
      • Example: “This app does not share on your behalf on Facebook
    • Pitfall 2: Obscuring Actual Information Flow
      • Avoidance: inform the user when information is being used, shared, etc.
      • Bad practice example: Google voice search; Good practice examples below
  • Concerning Action
    • Pitfall 3: Emphasizing Configuration over Action
      • “Designs should not require excessive configuration to create and maintain privacy. They should enable users to practice privacy management as a natural consequence of their ordinary use of the system”.
      • Privacy management should be integrated into the natural workflow of users so that they don’t have to think about it.
      • Avoidance: embed privacy configurations into the actions necessary to use the system.
      • Examples: mobile app permissions and Facebook’s post visibility
    • Pitfall 4: Lacking Coarse-Grained Control
      • “Designs should offer an obvious, top-level mechanism for halting and resuming the disclosure of personal information.”
      • Bad practice example: it is not straight forward to momentarily disable search history on Amazon, which sometimes results in many irrelevant recommendations.
      • Good practice example: Instead of specifying each and everyone who can/cannot see your Facebook post, group the audience under lists, or pre-defined labels like “friends”, “friends of friends”, “public”, etc..
    • Pitfall 5: Inhibiting Established Practice
      • “Designs should beware inhibiting existing social practice.”
      • Good practice example: Phone companies can identify your location, but they do not show it to the parties you are communicating with.

Privacy space framework by Benjamin Brunk

Features of privacy solutions:

  • Awareness
  • Detection
  • Prevention
  • Response
  • Recovery

Privacy by Awareness

  • Awareness features are informative and help users monitor what is going on.4
  • Aim: inform the user
  • Example:
    • Display information about trackers on current webpage, whether location is being sent

Privacy by Detection

  • Tools or features that scan or actively look for potential problems. Often, detection tools are always running in the background; a virus scanner is a common detection tool that looks for malware and for certain dangerous operations being performed in memory.
  • Aim: Actively look for problems in the background
  • Example:
    • Find trackers on current webpage
    • Virus scanner
    • Malware detection

Privacy by Prevention

  • A feature or tool that is used as a precaution.
  • Aim: Prevent a potential compromise of privacy.
  • Example:
    • Encryption.
    • Digital signatures.
    • Secure deletion of sensitive documents.
    • Anonymity tools.

Privacy by Response

  • Taking action after a problem has been detected is a response.
  • Aim: Take action to mitigate further privacy problems.
  • Example:
    • Cancelling credit card after an unauthorized payment.
    • Blocking network traffic from a certain IP

Privacy by Recovery

  • Features and tools that help you get back to normal.
  • Example:
    • Restoring to the last known good state.
    • Patching bugs that allowed intruders to gain unauthorized access.
    • Reinstalling corrupted files.

Challenges of Implementing Privacy Tools

  • Conveying the purpose of the tool
  • Conveying current privacy state
  • Configuration
  • Avoiding errors, failing safe

Major problems found by Leon et al. in most privacy tools

  • Communication problems and confusing interfaces
  • Inappropriate defaults
  • Need for feedback
  • Users want protections that do not break websites

Learned lessons

  • Usable privacy has the same requirements as usable security
  • Same usability metrics apply to privacy e.g., efficiency, learnability, memorability and user preference

Don’t forget the other side of the story

  • Lawgivers argue that people might
    • Abuse privacy to do bad things
    • Commit fraud, evade taxes
    • Commit terrorism without being detected
  • The challenges here are:
    • How to balance privacy and accountability?
    • How to balance individual privacy and societal security?
    • How to build systems that support this balance?
